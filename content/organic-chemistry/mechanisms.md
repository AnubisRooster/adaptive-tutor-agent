# Reaction Mechanisms

A **mechanism** is the step-by-step account of how bonds break and form during a reaction. Mastering mechanisms means you can predict products you've never seen, rather than memorizing reactions.

## Curved-Arrow Notation

Curved arrows track the movement of **electrons**, not atoms. A full-headed arrow shows a pair of electrons moving:

- The **tail** starts at the electron source (a lone pair or a bond).
- The **head** points to where the electrons go (forming a new bond or landing on an atom).

Arrows always flow from electron-rich (nucleophile) to electron-poor (electrophile).

## Nucleophiles and Electrophiles

- A **nucleophile** ("nucleus-loving") is electron-rich: it donates an electron pair. Examples: hydroxide, amines, alkenes.
- An **electrophile** ("electron-loving") is electron-poor: it accepts an electron pair. Examples: carbonyl carbons, carbocations, protons.

Nearly every organic reaction is a nucleophile attacking an electrophile. Learn to label both in any molecule and the mechanism often follows.

## Intermediates vs. Transition States

- A **transition state** is the highest-energy point along a step; it cannot be isolated.
- An **intermediate** (e.g., a carbocation) sits in an energy valley between steps and exists briefly.

Carbocation stability (3° > 2° > 1°) frequently decides which pathway dominates, because more stable intermediates form faster (Hammond's postulate).

## Thermodynamics vs. Kinetics

- **Thermodynamics** asks whether products are more stable than reactants (is the reaction favorable?).
- **Kinetics** asks how fast it goes (what is the activation energy?).

A reaction can be favorable yet slow. Catalysts lower activation energy without changing the overall energy difference.

## Why It Matters

Once you can push arrows from nucleophile to electrophile and rank intermediate stability, the major reaction families — substitution, elimination, addition — stop being separate facts and become variations on one logic.
