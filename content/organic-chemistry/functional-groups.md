# Functional Groups

A **functional group** is a specific arrangement of atoms that gives a molecule characteristic chemical behavior. Organic chemistry becomes manageable once you reason by functional group instead of memorizing individual compounds — molecules with the same group react in similar ways.

## Common Groups

- **Alkanes** (C–C, C–H only): relatively unreactive; the inert backbone.
- **Alkenes** (C=C) and **alkynes** (C≡C): electron-rich pi bonds act as nucleophiles in addition reactions.
- **Alcohols** (R–OH): polar, hydrogen-bonding; can be oxidized.
- **Ethers** (R–O–R): fairly inert, good solvents.
- **Amines** (R–NH₂): basic and nucleophilic due to nitrogen's lone pair.
- **Aldehydes** (R–CHO) and **ketones** (R–CO–R): the carbonyl (C=O) carbon is electrophilic.
- **Carboxylic acids** (R–COOH): acidic; the conjugate base (carboxylate) is resonance-stabilized.
- **Esters** (R–COO–R) and **amides** (R–CO–NH): carbonyl derivatives central to biology and polymers.

## The Carbonyl: A Key Pattern

The carbonyl group (C=O) appears in aldehydes, ketones, acids, esters, and amides. Oxygen is more electronegative than carbon, so it pulls electron density toward itself. This leaves the carbonyl **carbon partially positive (electrophilic)** and the **oxygen partially negative**. Most carbonyl chemistry is a nucleophile attacking that electrophilic carbon.

## Polarity and Properties

Functional groups also determine physical properties. Groups that hydrogen bond (alcohols, acids, amines) raise boiling points and water solubility. Nonpolar hydrocarbon regions do the opposite. A molecule's overall behavior is a tug-of-war between its polar groups and its nonpolar skeleton.

## Why It Matters

Identifying functional groups tells you, at a glance, where a molecule will react, whether it is acidic or basic, and how it will behave physically. It is the single most useful organizing principle in the subject.
