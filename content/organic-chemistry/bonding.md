# Bonding & Hybridization

Organic chemistry is the chemistry of carbon. Carbon's ability to form four covalent bonds — to other carbons and to many elements — lets it build chains, rings, and complex three-dimensional shapes.

## Covalent Bonds

A covalent bond is a shared pair of electrons. Carbon has four valence electrons, so it forms four bonds to reach a stable octet. Bonds can be single, double, or triple, corresponding to one, two, or three shared pairs.

## Hybridization

To explain molecular shapes, we mix carbon's 2s and 2p orbitals into equivalent **hybrid orbitals**:

- **sp³**: four single bonds, tetrahedral geometry, ~109.5° angles (e.g., methane, CH₄). All sigma bonds.
- **sp²**: one double bond plus two single bonds, trigonal planar, ~120° (e.g., ethene, the carbons in C=C). Three sigma bonds + one pi bond.
- **sp**: a triple bond or two double bonds, linear, 180° (e.g., ethyne, C≡C). Two sigma bonds + two pi bonds.

The number of hybrid orbitals equals the number of sigma bonds plus lone pairs on the atom.

## Sigma vs. Pi Bonds

- A **sigma (σ) bond** forms by head-on overlap of orbitals along the bond axis. It is strong and allows free rotation.
- A **pi (π) bond** forms by side-on overlap of unhybridized p orbitals, above and below the axis. It is weaker than a sigma bond and **prevents rotation**, which is why double bonds create rigid geometry and cis/trans isomers.

A single bond is one σ. A double bond is one σ + one π. A triple bond is one σ + two π.

## Why It Matters

Hybridization predicts shape, and shape governs how molecules fit together and react. The exposed, higher-energy electrons of pi bonds make double and triple bonds reactive sites (nucleophilic), while the strong sigma framework holds the molecule together. Recognizing hybridization at a glance is the foundation for everything that follows.
