# Ethics

Ethics asks what we ought to do and what makes actions right or wrong. Three major normative frameworks dominate the conversation.

## Consequentialism

Rightness depends on outcomes. **Utilitarianism** (Bentham, Mill) says the right act maximizes overall well-being (happiness minus suffering). Strengths: impartial, action-guiding. Challenges: it can seem to justify sacrificing individuals for the greater good, and measuring/aggregating well-being is hard.

## Deontology

Rightness depends on duties and rules, not just outcomes. Kant argued we should act only on maxims we could will to be **universal laws**, and always treat persons as **ends in themselves**, never merely as means. Strengths: protects individual rights. Challenges: rules can conflict, and rigid rule-following can yield bad outcomes.

## Virtue Ethics

Focuses on character rather than acts or rules. Aristotle asked what a flourishing human life (*eudaimonia*) looks like, cultivated through virtues (courage, honesty, temperance) found as a mean between extremes. Strengths: realistic about moral development. Challenges: gives less precise guidance in specific dilemmas.

## Metaethics

Steps back to ask what moral claims *are*. Are they objective facts (**moral realism**), expressions of attitude (**expressivism**), or culturally relative? Distinguish:
- **Normative ethics**: which acts are right?
- **Applied ethics**: how do principles apply to real cases (medicine, AI, war)?
- **Metaethics**: what is the nature and status of moral claims?

## Working With Dilemmas

A useful habit: analyze a hard case from each framework. A consequentialist weighs outcomes; a deontologist checks duties and rights; a virtue ethicist asks what a person of good character would do. Where they agree, you have strong reason; where they diverge, you have located the moral tension.
