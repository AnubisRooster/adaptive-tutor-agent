# Research Methods

Psychology is an empirical science: claims about mind and behavior are tested against systematic observation.

## Variables and Experiments

- The **independent variable (IV)** is manipulated by the researcher; the **dependent variable (DV)** is measured.
- A true **experiment** randomly assigns participants to conditions, which controls for confounds and lets us infer **causation**.
- **Correlational** studies measure relationships without manipulation. Key rule: **correlation does not imply causation** — a third variable, or reverse causation, may explain the link.

## Validity and Reliability

- **Internal validity**: are we confident the IV caused the change in the DV (no confounds)?
- **External validity**: do results generalize beyond the study (other people, settings)?
- **Reliability**: would the measurement give consistent results on repetition?
- **Construct validity**: does the measure actually capture the concept (e.g., does this test really measure "anxiety")?

## Common Pitfalls

- **Sampling bias**: a non-representative sample (e.g., only undergraduates) limits generalization.
- **Demand characteristics**: participants guess the hypothesis and change behavior.
- **Experimenter bias**: researchers unconsciously influence results — countered by *double-blind* designs.
- **Placebo effects**: improvement from expectation alone — countered by control groups.

## Statistics Basics

- **Descriptive statistics** summarize data (mean, median, standard deviation).
- **Inferential statistics** estimate whether an effect is likely real rather than chance. A **p-value** is the probability of the observed data (or more extreme) if there were no real effect; small p-values suggest the effect is unlikely to be due to chance alone.
- **Effect size** tells you how *big* an effect is, which matters as much as whether it is statistically significant.
- The **replication crisis** reminds us that single studies are weak evidence; converging results across studies are what build knowledge.
