# Cognition

Cognitive psychology studies the mental processes behind perception, attention, memory, reasoning, and decision-making.

## Attention

Attention is a limited resource. **Selective attention** filters relevant from irrelevant input (the "cocktail party effect"). **Inattentional blindness** shows we miss unattended events even when they are obvious (the invisible gorilla study). Multitasking is largely rapid task-switching, which carries a measurable cost.

## Perception

Perception is constructive: the brain infers a model of the world from incomplete sensory data, using prior expectations. Visual illusions reveal these inferences. Top-down (expectation-driven) and bottom-up (data-driven) processing interact constantly.

## Reasoning and Heuristics

Humans often use **heuristics** — fast mental shortcuts that are usually useful but sometimes biased (Kahneman & Tversky):
- **Availability**: judging probability by how easily examples come to mind.
- **Representativeness**: judging by similarity to a prototype, ignoring base rates.
- **Anchoring**: relying too heavily on the first number encountered.
- **Confirmation bias**: seeking evidence that supports existing beliefs.

Dual-process theory distinguishes **System 1** (fast, automatic, intuitive) from **System 2** (slow, effortful, deliberate).

## Decision-Making

Expected-utility models assume people weigh probabilities and values rationally, but **prospect theory** shows we are loss-averse (losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good) and evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point rather than in absolute terms.

## Why It Matters

Understanding cognition explains everyday errors, informs better interface and policy design ("choice architecture"), and connects directly to how learning and memory work.
