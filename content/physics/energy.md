# Energy & Momentum

Energy and momentum are powerful because they are **conserved**: in an isolated system, the totals do not change, which lets us solve problems without tracking every force over time.

## Work and Energy

- **Work**: `W = F·d·cos(θ)` — force times displacement along the force's direction. Units: joules (J).
- **Kinetic energy**: `KE = ½·m·v²` — energy of motion.
- **Gravitational potential energy**: `PE = m·g·h` near Earth's surface.
- **Work–energy theorem**: the net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy.

## Conservation of Energy

In the absence of friction and other losses, **mechanical energy** (KE + PE) is constant. A ball rolling down a ramp trades PE for KE. When friction is present, some mechanical energy converts to thermal energy, but *total* energy is still conserved.

Strategy: identify the system at two moments and set `KE1 + PE1 = KE2 + PE2 (+ energy lost)`.

## Momentum

- **Momentum**: `p = m·v`, a vector.
- **Impulse**: `J = F·Δt = Δp` — a force over time changes momentum. This is why airbags (longer Δt) reduce the force on you.

## Conservation of Momentum

In a collision with no external forces, total momentum before equals total momentum after.
- **Elastic** collisions conserve both momentum and kinetic energy (e.g., ideal billiard balls).
- **Inelastic** collisions conserve momentum but not kinetic energy; in a **perfectly inelastic** collision the objects stick together.

## Choosing the Right Tool

If a problem asks about speeds and heights without time, energy conservation is usually fastest. If it involves collisions or forces over a time interval, reach for momentum and impulse. Recognizing which conserved quantity applies is the key skill.
