# Genetics & Inheritance

Genetics explains how traits pass from one generation to the next and how the instructions for building an organism are stored and copied.

## DNA, Genes, and Chromosomes

- **DNA** is a double helix of nucleotides; the bases pair specifically: A–T and G–C. This complementary pairing is the key to copying information faithfully.
- A **gene** is a segment of DNA that codes for a product (usually a protein).
- **Chromosomes** are long DNA molecules wrapped around proteins. Humans have 23 pairs (46 total) — one of each pair from each parent.

## Alleles and Genotype vs. Phenotype

- An **allele** is a version of a gene. You inherit two alleles per gene, one from each parent.
- **Genotype** is the genetic makeup (e.g., Bb); **phenotype** is the observable trait (e.g., brown eyes).
- **Dominant** alleles mask **recessive** ones. A recessive trait appears only when both alleles are recessive (bb).

## Mendelian Inheritance

Gregor Mendel's pea experiments revealed predictable ratios. A cross between two heterozygotes (Bb × Bb) yields a **3:1** phenotype ratio and a **1:2:1** genotype ratio. A **Punnett square** maps the possible combinations of alleles from each parent.

## Meiosis and Variation

**Meiosis** produces gametes (sperm and eggs) with half the chromosome number, so fertilization restores the full set. Two processes generate variation: **independent assortment** (chromosomes sort randomly) and **crossing over** (homologous chromosomes swap segments). This shuffling is why siblings differ.

## Beyond Simple Mendelian Rules

Most real traits are more complex: **polygenic** (height, skin color — many genes), **incomplete dominance** (blended phenotypes), **codominance** (both alleles expressed, like AB blood type), and **environmental influence**. Genes set possibilities; environment shapes outcomes.

## Why It Matters

Genetics links cells to evolution: heritable variation is the raw material natural selection acts on. It also underpins medicine, agriculture, and biotechnology.
