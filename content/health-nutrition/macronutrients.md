# Macronutrients

Macronutrients are the nutrients the body needs in large amounts and that provide energy: **carbohydrates, proteins, and fats**. (Water is sometimes included as a macronutrient, but it provides no calories.)

## Carbohydrates (~4 kcal/g)

Carbs are the body's preferred quick energy source, broken down to glucose. They range from simple sugars to complex starches and fiber.
- **Complex carbs and fiber** (whole grains, legumes, vegetables) digest slowly, giving steadier blood sugar and feeding gut bacteria.
- **Added sugars** spike blood glucose and provide energy with few other nutrients.
- **Fiber** is a carbohydrate humans can't digest; it aids satiety, digestion, and cholesterol control.

## Proteins (~4 kcal/g)

Proteins are chains of **amino acids** used to build and repair tissues, enzymes, hormones, and immune molecules. Of the 20 amino acids, **nine are essential** (must come from food). "Complete" proteins (animal products, soy, quinoa) contain all nine; plant proteins can be combined to cover them all. Typical needs are roughly 0.8 g/kg body weight for sedentary adults, higher for athletes.

## Fats (~9 kcal/g)

Fats store energy densely, build cell membranes, and carry fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).
- **Unsaturated fats** (olive oil, nuts, fish) are generally heart-healthy. Omega-3s are essential.
- **Saturated fats** (fatty meat, butter) are fine in moderation.
- **Trans fats** (industrial partially hydrogenated oils) are harmful and largely phased out.

## Balance, Not Dogma

No single macronutrient is "bad." Health depends on overall pattern: quality of sources, total energy, and individual needs and goals. A useful habit is asking not just "how many grams" but "from what food, and how processed?"

## Correlation vs. Causation

Much nutrition news comes from observational studies that show associations, not proof. Treat dramatic single-study headlines with caution; trust the weight of evidence across many studies.
