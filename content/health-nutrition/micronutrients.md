# Micronutrients

Micronutrients — **vitamins and minerals** — are needed in small amounts but are essential for nearly every body process. They provide no energy themselves but enable the reactions that use energy.

## Vitamins

- **Fat-soluble (A, D, E, K)**: stored in body fat and the liver, so excess can accumulate. Vitamin D, made in skin from sunlight, supports bone and immune health and is commonly low in winter climates.
- **Water-soluble (B-complex and C)**: not stored much, so they're needed regularly and excess is excreted. B vitamins drive energy metabolism; vitamin C supports collagen and immune function and acts as an antioxidant.

## Minerals

- **Macrominerals** (calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, potassium, sodium): needed in larger amounts for bones, nerves, and fluid balance.
- **Trace minerals** (iron, zinc, iodine, selenium): needed in tiny amounts but critical. Iron carries oxygen in blood; iodine is required for thyroid hormones.

## Deficiencies and Excesses

Classic deficiencies illustrate why these matter: scurvy (vitamin C), rickets (vitamin D), anemia (iron), goiter (iodine). But more is not always better — fat-soluble vitamins and some minerals are toxic in excess. Supplements help correct genuine shortfalls but rarely improve health in people who are already replete.

## Food First

Whole foods deliver micronutrients alongside fiber and beneficial compounds, and in forms the body absorbs well. A varied diet rich in vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains, and (if eaten) animal foods covers most needs. Specific groups — pregnant people, older adults, vegans, those with limited sun — may need targeted supplements.

## Bioavailability

How much of a nutrient you actually absorb varies. For example, vitamin C boosts iron absorption from plant foods, while calcium can inhibit it. Pairing foods thoughtfully matters as much as the raw numbers on a label.
