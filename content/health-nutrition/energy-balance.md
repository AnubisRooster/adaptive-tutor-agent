# Energy Balance & Metabolism

Energy balance compares the calories you take in from food with the calories you expend. Over time it largely determines whether body weight rises, falls, or holds steady — though the system is more dynamic than simple arithmetic.

## Components of Energy Expenditure

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (**TDEE**) has several parts:

- **Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)**: energy to keep you alive at rest (breathing, circulation, cell maintenance). This is the largest chunk, often 60–70%.
- **Thermic Effect of Food (TEF)**: energy used to digest and process food (~10%). Protein has the highest TEF.
- **Physical activity**: both exercise and **NEAT** (non-exercise activity — fidgeting, walking, posture), which varies a lot between people.

## A Calorie Is Not the Whole Story

"Calories in vs. calories out" is true at the level of physics, but the body adapts: BMR can fall during prolonged dieting, hunger hormones shift, and NEAT changes unconsciously. Food quality also affects appetite and how many calories you end up eating. So the equation is real but its terms are not fixed.

## Metabolism Myths

- Metabolism doesn't "break" from eating normally; large, lasting metabolic damage is rare.
- There is no food that meaningfully "speeds up" metabolism enough to matter for weight.
- Muscle burns somewhat more energy at rest than fat, but the effect is modest.

## Practical Framing

Sustainable change usually comes from habits that make energy balance easier without constant counting: prioritizing protein and fiber for satiety, staying active, sleeping well (poor sleep raises appetite), and managing stress. Extreme restriction tends to backfire.

## Individual Variation

Two people of the same size can have meaningfully different TDEE due to genetics, muscle mass, hormones, and activity. General formulas (e.g., Mifflin-St Jeor for BMR) give estimates, not exact prescriptions — adjust based on real-world results.
